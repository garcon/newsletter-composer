## Everyone builds their own software

![A generated illustration shows a young woman at a bar served by an androgynous robot. The woman says: I imagine something like my personal dashboard.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/00ae18e2-016f-438b-b08d-be5e4c249405_724x478.webp)

Last week another twenty multimedia students in Martin’s course learned how to [build interactive websites using only prompts](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7415163006310932480/) given to *GitHub Copilot* in *VS Code*. The results were web apps using databases and deployed on platforms like *Vercel*.

The aim of the vibecoding exercise was to teach future multimedia creators that they can prototype ideas directly in code instead of in *Figma*. The side–effect was that students realised coding AI agents can solve needs for which there are no ready‑made products. They fell for the new trend of micro‑apps.

It’s not accidental. [Vibecoding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vibe-coding) and AI‑powered micro‑apps change software from something we download into something we assemble ourselves. This shift affects creation, distribution and maintenance of tools and transforms the role of web professionals. Tools like *Cursor* or *Claude Code* let you describe intent in natural language and get a working application. Vibecoding drives the rise of micro‑apps: small, highly personal tools for one person, one situation, one problem.

This is primarily a behavioural change rather than a purely technological novelty. Instead of asking “Which app should I download?”, the question becomes “What if I build it myself?” These tools generally aren’t aimed at mass markets or monetisation. They appear because a spreadsheet is no longer enough and a commercial product would be unnecessarily heavy. They fill the gap between document and software. A typical example is *[GSC Opportunity Mapper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gsc-opportunity-mapper)*, which we wrote about last week.

For now the main bottleneck that prevents wider adoption of micro‑apps is deployment. Platforms that combine generation, hosting and publication allow an idea to become a web app in minutes and an AI agent can deploy on behalf of the user — who often doesn’t know how the app got on the web. Easy sharing makes the web a natural distribution layer again. App stores with fees, approvals and restrictions are ill‑suited to personal, ad‑hoc creation.

But there are risks. AI‑generated code often contains security flaws and structural weaknesses. People without formal training may not be able to assess what they publish, especially if apps handle sensitive data. Another problem is maintenance and evolution.

A new role for specialists is emerging. People bring ready prototypes created with AI. Real value shifts to cultivating these tools for clarity, accessibility, security and long‑term sustainability. Less building from scratch, more expert judgement.

Software becomes malleable material. Once people get used to tools adapting to them, their expectations and approaches to software will change completely, and there will be no going back.

## Linkodrome

**How to effectively improve visual design** through practical training, repetition and mindful reflection is explored by *Filippos Protogeridis*. [Start by mastering the basics](https://blog.designary.com/p/the-practical-guide-to-improving) (typography, colour, hierarchy), study design languages (e.g. Apple HIG, Material 3) and practise by imitating existing interfaces, remixing your work or creating concepts outside brief constraints. Filippos emphasises that visual skills are developed through repetition and noticing differences.

**Design tokens are portable units of design decisions,** connecting designers and developers across platforms. *Francesco Improta* explains the [difference between code and tokens](https://designtokens.substack.com/p/using-design-tokens-as-variables) and outlines best practices for using them in Figma, CSS and Sass. Focus on clear semantics, version control, structured naming and automation.

![An alarm on a phone offers the option to snooze for $10.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ccee7b95-139c-4e93-9be5-9d795071d539_949x2048.jpeg)
*Pay to Snooze might be the next big business hit.*

**Figma isn’t dead, but the designer’s role is changing.** *Thomas Budiman* responds to the provocation that Figma has lost its meaning and develops a deeper reflection on AI’s impact on creative work. He notes that [tools change but design as a process remains](https://designbuddy.substack.com/p/is-figma-dead). AI shifts boundaries and lowers barriers, which benefits juniors, while seniors keep space where intuition and understanding are needed.

**The job market for designers in 2026 has fundamentally changed the rules.** Companies seek specialists skilled in AI with clear business impact, not generalists, writes *Tom Scott*. [Show outcomes, strategic thinking and adaptability.](https://verifiedinsider.substack.com/p/the-playbook-to-finding-a-design) Portfolios must be targeted and measurable, supported by prepared videos, presentations and concise summaries. Personal branding and visibility on networks are paths to opportunities.

> “If you want to stand out as a designer, please get yourself a custom domain and email address. out of the 575 applicants, only 28 had both.”\
> — [Jesse Vermeulen](https://x.com/jesse_vermeulen/status/2011736102210806130?s=12)

**The shift in design systems from static components to adaptive interfaces** is a trend described by *Yesenia Perez‑Cruz*. The new generation of UIs must focus on product primitives — objects users create (e.g. a task, a discount) — and adapt visual language and behaviour across contexts. AI can use context (intent, work phase, permissions) to dynamically alter interfaces based on user experience and needs.

**Just‑in‑Time UI** introduces a design approach that offers dynamic, context‑sensitive interfaces rather than static screens, writes *Andrew Sims*. With AI, interfaces become flexible, proactive and capable of guiding a conversation rather than simply reacting. [The user experience shifts from predetermined paths to live interactions](https://signalpath.substack.com/p/just-in-time-interfaces), adapting to needs, intent and situation in real time.

**Jakob Nielsen’s 2026** predicts a shift from the miracle phase of AI to integration. UX, not model intelligence, becomes the advantage. AI agents, generative UIs (GenUI) and physical AI in robots and autonomous vehicles are developing. Multimodality changes content creation rules while the compute [crisis deepens divides between paying users and those on free tiers](https://jakobnielsenphd.substack.com/p/2026-predictions).

**15 ways accessibility benefits everyone:** Accessible design makes sites clearer, faster to use, reduces cognitive load and increases trust and conversions. It improves readability, reduces errors and eases work and learning. Companies also [gain from a wider audience and lower support costs](https://a11ywithdiana.substack.com/p/15-benefits-of-accessible-web). Accessibility is a strategic decision with measurable benefits.

![A chart shows the steady decline of Stack Overflow questions since 2016, with COVID‑19 as a temporary spike. Since the rise of AI the fall is steep almost to zero.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/5308e659-15e0-47ed-b1b8-cfddefa97f8a_1346x1144.jpeg)

**It’s time to reconsider UX metrics.** Traditional UX metrics like task success or user satisfaction are no longer enough. Result‑focused [metrics that tie to business outcomes](https://uxuniversity.io/p/how-to-set-ux-metrics-that-matter) are important, shared across teams and tracked over time. UX is not about the number of tests but about the change those tests drive.

**Card decks help with writing, research and leadership.** Three popular packs: *Storyteller Tactics*, *Laws of UX Cards* and *The Leader’s Toolkit* help *Andrea Chiarelli* overcome blank‑page syndrome, focus and act effectively. [Each deck provides concrete tools](https://andreachiarelli.substack.com/p/thinking-with-decks): from crafting strong narratives to improving UX to everyday managerial situations.

*[Jeffrey Zeldman](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jeffrey-zeldman)* turned seventy‑one. In an era when the web depended on proprietary solutions he championed web standards, connecting the community through initiatives such as *A List Apart*, *Web Standards Project* and *An Event Apart* to promote knowledge sharing.

*[GSC Opportunity Mapper](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/gsc-opportunity-mapper)* converts exports from Google Search Console into an actionable SEO plan, helping to prioritise content by potential and flag weaknesses such as competing pages for the same query, low CTR or missing internal links.

[HTML](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/html) is the language that defines meaning and structure of web content. It describes what is content, not how it looks or behaves. Semantics serve accessibility and SEO. HTML was born from *Tim Berners‑Lee*’s vision and its evolution is now guided by W3C.

[Conversational UI](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/conversational-ui) lets users control a web service using natural language instead of classic menus. Language models increase its flexibility but don’t make it magic. It must work with context, be transparent and handle errors. Good interfaces combine free text with guided elements, reducing mistakes and aiding orientation.

[Content delivery network](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/cdn) (CDN) is a distributed network of servers that delivers web content as close as possible to users. It shortens load times, reduces origin server load and increases stability under heavy conditions. It acts as a cache and mainly serves static content.
