## Codeless isn't no‑code

For a decade the terms *no‑code* and *low‑code* described low‑barrier approaches to building software. They typically provide a visual way to assemble automation or simple applications; the output is frequently configuration within a platform rather than portable source code.

With the arrival of agentic AI and *[vibecoding](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vibe-coding)* a different approach to programming has emerged: the author need not know a programming language, and may not even be able to describe an algorithm at the level expected by traditional no‑code tools, yet can still produce software with source code.

*Anil Dash* proposed a new name for this approach: *[codeless](https://www.anildash.com/2026/01/22/codeless/)*. The key difference between codeless and no‑code lies in control and long‑term sustainability. No‑code emphasises accessibility and speed within a platform’s constraints.

Codeless, in its agentic form, emphasises leverage: a human specifies intent and constraints, and AI agents generate conventional source code. The primary artefact is a repository, not a platform configuration. That makes versioning, reviews, independent deployment and long‑term ownership possible.

There are different demands. No‑code lowers the barrier to entry but limits possibilities. Codeless reduces routine work but requires disciplined thinking, architectural sense and quality‑oriented practice.

![Meme](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8bc1f62a-e706-4388-8f42-b89fca9a7d7b_800x533.webp)

*Codeless programmers orchestrate AI agents.*

Is the term codeless needed if we already have vibecoding? Both vibecoding and codeless use AI for software creation, but they differ in control and responsibility. Vibecoding is exploratory and intuitive: people describe intent and iterate, producing quick prototypes. Codeless in the agentic sense is a disciplined process: humans articulate requirements, constraints and architecture, and agents generate and refine real code with a focus on quality and maintainability.

## Linkodrome

**Unicorns return.** *Jakob Nielsen* used to be sceptical of UX unicorns. Now, aided by AI, generalist designers who cover research to content strategy are becoming more capable. AI narrows the gap between experts and novices, raising productivity and quality in many projects. Where UX unicorn once implied compromise, AI now helps deliver competent design across a range of tasks.\

**SEO isn’t dead.** Claims that SEO dies because of AI are exaggerated. Studies from *Graphite.io* and *Similarweb* show organic search traffic in the US fell only 2.5% year‑on‑year, not the drastic drops sometimes reported. The biggest and smallest sites grew while mid‑sized sites saw pressure; search remains a primary traffic source.\

![Satire about calendar invitations showing how late attendees will be.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/2349d715-975d-4d03-a0d2-b54866cd19d5_1920x1920.png)

*Waiting for Godot.*

**Efficient SEO data processing without the cloud.** *Marek Prokop* shared an elegant approach to process millions of URLs from Screaming Frog using DuckDB. It replaces heavyweight tools like BigQuery. DuckDB is a local, fast analytical database. Marek shows how to export crawl CSVs, import them into DuckDB and analyse with SQL or via AI tools such as Claude Desktop with an MCP server.\

**Ten rules for better design critique.** *Geoff Hemeon* summarises ten principles for constructive, safe design critique: trust, separating the work from identity, and formulating concrete feedback focused on context and craft.

**Why Agile and UX still don’t get along.** Agile emphasises fast iteration and meeting business requirements; UX stresses deep understanding of real user needs. *Charles Lambdin* argues that Agile often replaces strategy with efficiency and neglects research, resulting in limited UX influence over product direction.\

**Pixel perfect slows modern web development.** *Amit Sheen* critiques pixel‑perfect as a leftover of print design that doesn’t fit adaptive interfaces, varied devices and living design systems. He advocates intent, design tokens and fluid units over fixed pixel values.

> “It’s absolutely insane to me that we’re 3+ years into a radical cutting of UX jobs and yet we’re also in the midst of a huge technology shift that really, really needs designers and user researchers.” — **Dan Saffer**

**What does a product manager actually do?** *Stephen P. Anderson* proposes a framework centred on risk and prioritisation, clarifying unknowns and working within constraints. At the core are activities that shape the main user experience and product vision. Other roles and responsibilities are summarised by *Teo Yu Siang* and *James Newhook*.\

**The future of product design:** Designers must combine aesthetic sense with systems thinking, rapid iteration and cross‑disciplinary collaboration. Roles shift towards curation of AI‑generated content and real‑time product shaping.\

> “Navigating today’s UX job market isn’t about applying harder. It’s about approaching your career strategically.” — **Jared Spool**

**Agentic shopping will reshape e‑commerce.** Universal Commerce Protocol offers a communicative interface for AI agents to interact directly with shops, enabling agentic commerce—shopping without clicks.\

**Icons that animate on :hover.** *Abhijit Jha* made an icon set that triggers a subtle animation on hover, signalling interactivity before action occurs.

[Dev stack](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/dev-stack) defines the technologies and tools that determine how software is built and maintained. Long‑term success depends on resilience: maintainability, performance, accessibility and security.

[No‑code and low‑code platforms](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/no-code) change how websites and apps are created, offering rapid prototyping and lower entry costs. Risks emerge over time: visual logic is harder to manage, vendor lock‑in grows and security issues can arise from misconfiguration.
