## Direct design and prompt‑to‑UI approaches devalue the craft in clients’ eyes

> **Direct design** – a way of creating interfaces where a designer describes intent and AI directly produces and edits the interface in code or a design tool.

AI tools shorten the time from idea to design and finished products. Designers can sketch flows and screens into rough prototypes with prompts in hours if they have good UI component libraries. It doesn’t much matter whether designers now prototype directly in code with AI or use [MCP tools](https://github.com/southleft/figma-console-mcp) to manipulate objects in Figma or [Pencil.dev](https://www.pencil.dev/).

Speed brings new risks. When designs come from prompts, a designer may be unable to explain why an interface works that way. Such outputs lose visible value for clients.

If it used to be true that a designer who can’t code can’t think, it’s now truer than ever. Added to that is the skill of describing flows and interfaces with formalised models—or at least understanding static and dynamic models of applications generated from prompts. And of course good design systems or UI kits remain the foundation.

## Linkodrome

**The first hundred active users are more valuable than 10,000 downloads.** Early active users reveal weaknesses, ambiguities and features that make sense. Their feedback helps improve onboarding, simplify processes and decide what to develop or cut. Instead of chasing marketing growth, [focus on learning and iteration](https://medium.com/@floydjsmith02/your-apps-first-100-users-matter-more-than-your-first-10-000-downloads-f6106a2b769f). 

**Figma fundamentally changed how digital products are designed.** Where files used to be sent between team members, [Figma introduced real‑time collaboration](https://medium.com/@quicktask.net/how-figma-changed-modern-product-design-workflows-73b5e6841304) in a shared environment. Designers can work on the same file simultaneously, follow each other’s cursors, comment on designs and iterate quickly. That reduces version chaos and lengthy handovers. The result is faster development, better communication and more effective distributed teams.

**How to turn AI into a product team.** *Shashank Kumar* describes why he stopped using AI as an assistant and started organising it like [a product team with clear roles](https://theshashankkumar.medium.com/i-turned-claude-into-a-product-organization-and-it-changed-how-i-build-74fcecdf9fe9): UX, UI, research, business analysis and product management. Such architecture reduces “decision debt”, involves monetisation during design and enables broader yet coherent exploration.

**Questioning fundamentals helps designers find the real problem.** It breaks issues down to basic truths and refuses to follow conventions blindly. It helps designers understand real user needs rather than copying existing solutions. It rests on repeatedly asking “why” until the core problem emerges.

**Great apps work without instructions.** Design should focus on the user’s primary goal and guide them to it as directly as possible. The interface must give immediate feedback, behave consistently and avoid unnecessary complexity. Usability testing with real users reveals where people hesitate or get lost. Intuitive UX comes from deep understanding of human behaviour, not flashy visuals.

**Why many products fail before launch.** Most products [fail before launch](https://medium.com/@Muslimahhh/why-most-products-fail-before-they-even-launch-1dfb98e7fc49) when teams poorly define the problem they aim to solve. Instead of understanding users, teams jump to features, roadmaps and technology. The result is a well‑built product that addresses the wrong problem.

> “User‑created content just covers the gap between ads.”\
> **— [Jean François Porchez](https://bsky.app/profile/typofonderie.com/post/3mg5sa2a6wk2a)**

**When a product becomes a place: a new view on UX and user behaviour.** *Dalia Katan* suggests thinking about product design architecturally. Her ARCH framework (Atmosphere, Route, Cue, Habit) helps align strategy with concrete interface details and maintain the overall experience.

**AI forces UXers back to the fundamentals: define the right problem.** According to *Jennifer Rousey*, AI refocuses UX design on its original purpose. Real value shifts to defining problems, understanding users and formulating intent. In the AI era the key skill becomes [describing needs accurately](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/ai-is-forcing-ux-back-to-basics-80073ef7c0cb).

**Where insight gets lost.** Research findings usually disappear gradually during handovers between tools and roles. Context, evidence and nuance fade. The issue is systemic: research and design tools rarely connect work. When designers lack direct access to research, they rely on intuition.

**Practical guide to contrasting colour palettes:** *Stéphanie Walter* dispels the myth that accessibility rules severely limit colour choices. The problem lies in unfamiliarity with contrast rules. Her step‑by‑step article explains creating palettes in Figma, checking contrast (4.5:1 for text) and documenting combinations.
