## People want to spend time, not save it

The biggest misconception around AI is that every interface should be as fast as possible. Sometimes that’s true: when someone needs to book accommodation, buy a ticket or sign up, they want to get it done quickly. In those moments, every unnecessary delay is harmful.

But much of the digital world is not a to‑do list. People often want to browse, compare, explore and linger until something catches their interest. They do not necessarily want to save time; they want to spend it enjoyably.

That is why browsing‑based interfaces remain powerful. The difference between searching and discovering matters. Search works when you know what you want. Discovery works when you don’t. Pure conversational interfaces can struggle because they demand a clear intent upfront. Feeds, galleries and recommendations show options first.

It’s not that people love inefficiency. It’s that they move between two modes. Sometimes they want to complete a task; other times they want to inhabit an experience. Good UX recognises which mode it serves. Reducing everything to instant completion can remove the very reason users came.

## Linkodrome

**Elements of User Experience.** *S. S. Karthik* explains the five‑layer UX model [via Jesse James Garrett](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jesse-james-garrett): Strategy, Scope, Structure, Skeleton and Surface. Each layer translates intent into a functional product.

**Practical UX research for solo designers:** *Sanjana Agarwal* shows how to analyse support tickets, speak to customers, check analytics, map journeys and rapidly test prototypes. Even small research yields essential user insights. Crucially, tie research outcomes directly to design decisions. [Read more](https://sanjana-agarwal.medium.com/the-solo-designers-guide-to-user-research-no-team-no-budget-no-problem-78fc7abd7923).

![Customer asks a fast‑food chain’s chat to write a Python script and it complies.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/2316c4e6-4a1d-4499-9c52-0a0d4f442fc6_780x1278.jpeg)

**If AI tool subscriptions feel pricey, try the Chipotle chat.**

**Claude Code for designers step‑by‑step.** *Tommaso Nervegna* demonstrates how Claude Code plus the Get Shit Done (GSD) methodology lets designers produce functional apps without traditional handoffs: the designer describes intent, AI generates code.

**Google announced Stitch,** an AI platform that converts natural language into finished designs. Designers praise its potential. Try it with MCP for integration.

> “A clock is often better than having to ask the time.”\
> **— Erika Hall**

**UX audits without heavy tools:** *Hopemidah Kendi* argues audits can rely on clear thinking rather than costly software. Start by defining user goals, simulate a new user’s journey and classify problems by severity.

**Patterns save time and nerves.** Design patterns, inspired by Christopher Alexander, form the backbone of design systems. They reduce cognitive load and improve communication within teams.

**Even a simple button is surprisingly complex.** *Liam Spradlin* explains how button states contribute to clarity: the user must understand both what a button will do and what it is doing now. Modern approaches combine visual cues—shape, colour, size—to communicate state effectively.
