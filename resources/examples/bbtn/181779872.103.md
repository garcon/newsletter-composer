## From The Designer

**Increase tappable areas.** Fingers (and cursors) don’t hit pixels.

❌ Tiny links and buttons stuck together\
✅ Target areas at least 44 × 44 px with spacing

👉 You’ll reduce mis‑taps.\
👉 Touch controls will become noticeably more comfortable.

Generous targets = fewer mistakes and less frustration.

## Linkodrome

**How to design irreversible and risky actions.** Beyond classic confirmation dialogs, *Victor Ponamariov* recommends techniques such as entering a keyword, inline confirmations, two‑phase verification, a second approver, delayed execution or offering an undo. Clarity, visual distinction of critical steps and understanding the target audience are essential. Victor emphasises that even best practices must be tested in context.

**Eight ways to think without templates.** Designers often suffer method fatigue when form precedes problem understanding. *Tom Eldridge* suggests eight modes of thinking – from prototyping to thinking about change – that better capture the cognitive nature of design and its real value.

**Accessibility is non‑negotiable.** *Declan Chidlow* expresses frustration that, despite legal pressure, accessibility is often treated as an optional cost rather than a fundamental human right. Accessibility improves the experience for everyone. Declan calls for personal responsibility – accessibility cannot be an afterthought.

**Practical programming principles for self‑taught developers.** *Kilian Valkhof* summarises principles that helped him write clearer, more maintainable code. He warns against premature optimisation and needless abstraction. The goal is readable, understandable, maintainable code, not only code that works.

**Don’t introduce process without a clear and inspiring UX vision.** *Jared Spool* shows that process alone breeds scepticism among stakeholders; people need to understand why change makes sense.

**Atomic Design and OOUX.** *Brad Frost* and *Sophia V. Prater* discuss evolving design systems and their role in complex digital environments. Brad reflects on his Pattern Lab origins and the concept of atomic design; with Sophia he explores the intersection of object‑oriented UX and design systems.

> “You can always go back, but no one will be there.”
> **— Tobias van Schneider**

**Designing AI interfaces:** Interfaces are disappearing as AI operates in the background. We must decide when technology should surface. Working with data is key – it enables personalisation but also carries ethical risks. The challenge is designing technologies that respect humanity and build trust.

**Six tested principles for AI interface design:** responsibility, mental‑model work, output variability, co‑creation, appropriate trust calibration and acceptance of imperfection. These principles were developed iteratively and tested with professional designers to help build safe, effective and meaningful AI products.

> “UX is like a jester at a royal court. There’s a lot of bright colours and delightful acts — but that’s not *the job* of a jester, merely their appearance.”
> **— Pavel Samsonov**

**Why do we burn out?** *Pavel Samsonov* reflects on burnout after leaving Amazon and argues that causes are systemic – toxic culture, poor leadership and meaningless goals drive exhaustion. Recovery comes from rethinking work relationships and goals, not just taking holidays.

**Why Japanese websites seem strange.** Westerners often find Japanese sites cluttered, but they follow local logic: thoroughness, clarity according to local priorities and aesthetics inspired by print media. Japanese web design reflects cultural specifics rather than technological backwardness.

## Don Norman brought design to everyday life

![Donald Arthur Norman (drawing based on a photograph)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/878335f3-9fba-4d14-ac58-13d5eda49616_800x533.webp)

“We must design for the way people behave, not for how we would wish them to behave.” — Donald A. Norman, *Living With Complexity*

[Don Norman] celebrated his 90th birthday at Christmas. He shifted responsibility for failures from users to design. When people fail, design failed first – a foundation for systematic analysis of interaction concepts such as affordances, signifiers and feedback.

## To be clear

User interfaces connect user intent with system reaction. UI is concrete, measurable and directly influenceable. Long‑term usability requires ongoing care, testing and adaptation. Empathy is a practical, research‑backed skill, but it needs distance and careful application to design within real constraints. Passkeys represent a major shift in authentication, eliminating passwords and phishing risk by leveraging public/private key cryptography.
