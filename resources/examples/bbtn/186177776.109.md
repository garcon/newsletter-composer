## Present an identity document

Age verification has moved from symbolic checkboxes to technical requirements. Legislators in the EU, the United Kingdom, Australia and parts of the US are forcing website creators to address the hard question: how to protect children without turning the internet into a mandatory identity checkpoint.

Regulators in major jurisdictions consider a simple user declaration ineffective and expect verifiable, auditable measures. The change affects not only adult content but the very architecture of the web, user experience and data practices.

Several assumptions must hold: technical readiness, a legal framework allowing precise age verification, and evidence from monitoring that users often bypass weak checks.

The EU’s Digital Services Act frames child protection as a systemic duty. The European Digital Identity Wallet points towards verifying the assertion “I am 18+” without transmitting identity — essentially, a flag of adulthood rather than personal data. Yet who learns where you verified your age?

The UK is moving more aggressively: Ofcom requires pornographic services to implement robust verification with real oversight and published outcomes.

Australia bans social accounts for under‑16s and shifts responsibility to platforms. France experiments with privacy‑friendly models that separate age verification from content access. The US remains fragmented — shaped by state laws and court signals.

Technically the field splits into three approaches: authoritative verification draws on trusted sources and offers high certainty at the cost of friction and data sensitivity; age estimation (often biometric) lowers barriers but introduces bias, errors and accessibility problems; credential‑based approaches aim to prove just the age attribute while minimising data, relying on interoperable, trusted governance.

UK data shows trade‑offs: after controls were introduced, visits to major sites fell while VPN usage rose. Gates change behaviour but may simply shift it elsewhere.

![Chart showing a drop in visits to adult sites in the UK alongside a large rise in VPN usage.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/464c9a43-a5ec-4d1f-bc41-460035a7ceb7_638x905.png)
*Brits stopped visiting porn sites but started using VPNs.* (The Economist)

A pragmatic path rejects false dilemmas. Child protection does not require universal identification. Good solutions are proportionate, data‑minimal, accessible and auditable — and sceptical of biometrics or the “upload your ID to every site” approach. Age verification becomes web infrastructure; whether it undermines or preserves user dignity depends on design choices made today.

## Linkodrome

**How Uber shares insight:** Uber created Kaleidoscope as a platform for circulating insights across the company. The goal was to connect people, processes and tools for better decision‑making, using a shared taxonomy rather than replacing existing systems.

**Sound as a missing layer in web design.** Sound can significantly improve interface perception. Raphael Salaja warns against misuse — loud autoplay, inappropriate effects, or lack of user control. Sound can offer faster and more emotional feedback than visuals, improving clarity and satisfaction when used sparingly and with respect for user preferences.

![Meme about password managers.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/63648934-7f53-4766-b496-6404ee06b16b_4096x3072.jpeg)

**How designers think:** Anthony Hobday notes that design is often curation more than creation, that good design is not always commercially successful and that high‑quality software often arises in small teams. Insights highlight that people perceive interfaces visually, culturally and emotionally.

**ChatGPT as a mirror of the past.** Josef Šlerka argues that large language models don’t create a new world but conserve past ideology — in particular, technocratic liberalism and middle‑class norms. LLMs are not objective intelligence but a statistical mirror of dominant culture, creating a class of AI gatekeepers who filter and edit outputs.

> “AI is our generation's radium — a discovery with genuinely useful applications in specific, controlled circumstances that we stupidly put in everything until we realised the harm far too late.”
> **— Nick Pettigrew**

**Three timeless books on design:** Holger Maassen recommends *Don’t Make Me Think* by Steve Krug, *Information Architecture for the Web and Beyond* by Morville and Rosenfeld, and *The Design of Everyday Things* by Don Norman.
