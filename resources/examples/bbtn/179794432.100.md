## From The Designer

**Limit the length of text lines.** Readability depends on paragraph width as well as font and spacing.

❌ Very long lines across the whole screen\
✅ Lines of roughly 60–80 characters

👉 Text is easier to scan with the eye.\
👉 Users are less likely to lose their place.

A sensible paragraph measure → more comfortable reading.

## Linkodrome

**One‑time codes are ending; passkeys replace them.** Traditional one‑time verification codes no longer offer sufficient protection against phishing – [security experts and major vendors are turning to passkeys](https://www.theregister.com/2025/12/06/multifactor_authentication_passkeys/). They rely on cryptographic keys and resist common attacks. Apple, Google and Microsoft support them, and adoption is growing rapidly. Companies report much shorter login times and fewer helpdesk incidents.

**UX design for crises.** *Vitaly Friedman* offers practical guidance on designing digital products that hold up under crisis and stress. He notes that [stress reduces people’s ability to think, decide and prioritise](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2025/11/designing-for-stress-emergency/), and design must accommodate that. He recommends limiting cognitive load, supporting clear steps and planning, testing products under realistic stress conditions and providing emergency modes.

**Precise prompts improve AI tool outputs.** With prototyping tools powered by AI, designers are learning to give clear prompts rather than doing manual work. Huei‑Hsin Wang argues that vague prompts fail: they produce chaotic, illogical and cluttered interfaces without hierarchy. AI lacks context and cannot decide like a human designer. For better results, Huei‑Hsin [recommends using visual references, specific keywords, sample data or code, and iterative feedback to the AI](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/vague-prototyping/).

**Usability rules in the era of generative AI.** Classic [Nielsen heuristics](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/usability-heuristics) are no longer sufficient. Generative AI changes interaction patterns. *Matt Soriano* proposes [10 new UX principles for AI](https://emesstyle.com/usability/). (The site may itself be AI‑generated, so you might have to click Gen AI and then Start Exploring – the links may not be very pretty.) Matt draws on research from the *Nielsen Norman Group* and the *Microsoft HAX Toolkit*.

> “The best tech innovation in the last couple of years is not AI, it’s the ‘undo’ button when you just sent an email and noticed you forgot the link/attachment.”
> **— [Stéphanie Walter](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7403445192143572992/)**

**Feedback doesn’t scale (and what to do about it).** Feedback loses effectiveness as teams grow beyond a certain size – typically around a hundred people. Carter Baxter explains how a once‑intimate conversation can become an impersonal avalanche of views without context, which feels like an attack. As companies scale, personal relationships erode, trust and motivation are filtered, and without structured systems for processing feedback leaders drown in noise. Solutions include delegated trust, structured channels and the ability to separate constructive critique from noise. *Honesty, transparency and humanity.*

**A recipe for a design review that everyone will love.** Designers at *Blinkist* faced a problem: regular design review meetings were being cancelled because no one wanted to present. Why? Stress, a need for perfect preparation and little useful feedback. The solution was a format change – from *critique* to *Design Open Hour*, where the goal is collaboration rather than judgement. Everyone participates and contributes ideas in Figma. [The new approach improved output quality and the willingness to meet.](https://uxdesign.cc/a-recipe-for-a-design-review-that-everyone-will-love-4991ab465772)

**GitHub Design System banned toast messages** because of accessibility concerns. *Adam Silver* [analyses four common objections](https://adamsilver.io/blog/can-you-make-toast-messages-accessible/) to the ban. Although some experts argue that toasts can be improved, Adam contends this misses the point: toasts disappear, distract users and are easy to overlook even with assistive technologies. GitHub supported its decision with user research.

**Line length will change how you think about layout.** *Andy Clarke* reflects on the typographic principle called the *measure* – line length – and shows how it fundamentally affects readability and overall composition of web layouts. Rather than adapting content to screen width, he suggests the opposite: let the text dictate the layout width. He proposes systems using CSS variables and logical properties such as `max-inline-size` or `column-width`, making measure the foundation of the design. When layout follows text, the result is more harmonious, natural and legible. Andy continues the tradition of typesetting practices.

**Anthropic’s new tool reveals people’s relationship with AI.** *[Anthropic Interviewer](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-interviewer)* used AI to run [1,250 interviews](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Anthropic/AnthropicInterviewer) with professionals about their experiences with artificial intelligence. Most participants view AI positively, as a productivity booster. There are concerns about replacement and loss of control. Creatives face stigma around AI use and economic uncertainty, while AI researchers remain sceptical for critical research tasks. The tool allows large‑scale qualitative research and deeper insight into how people actually use AI. [Try it yourself.](https://claude.ai/interviewer)

**Chromatic is a minimalist colour game** by designer *Kevin Feyder*. The goal is to assemble coloured tiles into a smooth gradient. Activating *relaxed mode* shows locked tiles to indicate correct positions as guidance. The *rotate hues* tool helps those with difficulty distinguishing colours. The game offers several grid sizes for different difficulty levels.

# Jared Spool taught designers that business outcomes matter

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/9416787d-9e36-417d-b679-85f4a7257ff7_1536x1024.webp)

*[Jared Spool](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jared-spool)* fundamentally changed how designers think about the web. Since the 1990s he has advocated that the web shouldn’t primarily be an expression of technical capability or an author’s artistic vision, but should be based on user behaviour and needs.

> “Good design, when it’s done well, becomes invisible.”
> **— [Jared Spool](https://talks.ui-patterns.com/videos/the-curious-properties-of-intuitive-web-pages-jared-spool)**

With his research organisation [UIE](https://aycl.uie.com/) he demonstrated that simple usability testing with real users uncovers major issues that internal teams miss.

Jared helped establish data‑driven design as a standard in leading web projects worldwide. His later work on strategic UX and [outcome‑driven design](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kWNzDES1zk4) shifted the field from asking “Is the site usable?” to “Are we achieving business outcomes?”

> [Jared Spool as a moral compass for UX](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/jared-spool)
> Jared Spool (*8 December 1960*) is one of the most influential figures in user experience. As founder of Center Centre – UIE he has devoted more than forty years to helping organisations understand what it truly means to measure design quality.

## To be clear

[Plain Old Semantic HTML](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/posh) is a return to basics: write source HTML so it carries meaning. Developers prefer semantic tags over presentational classes because they ensure accessibility, search‑engine understanding and long‑term maintainability.

[SARAH](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/sarah) (Shock, Anger, Rejection, Acceptance, Help) describes emotional phases users go through during digital product changes. It draws on Elisabeth Kübler‑Ross’s stages of grief and helps designers understand why people often react negatively to new interfaces. Designers applying this framework can turn change into a smooth transition and improve adoption.

[Vyfikundace](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/vyfikundace) is a playful Czech term for seemingly clever improvements that easily become unnecessary complications. In digital design it names tweaks that start as innovations but end in frustration — they slow loading, confuse users and break the sense of an interface.

[Conformity bias](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/conformity-bias) is the tendency to uncritically follow the majority. Designers harness conformity via elements like ratings, leaderboards or “X people are currently watching”. When used honestly, it helps people decide and builds trust. When abused — for example with fake numbers or invented reviews — it risks trust and legal consequences.
