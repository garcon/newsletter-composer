## The paradox of building your own tool

![Jakub assembling littleBits pieces. (AI‑generated drawing)](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f0590415-5625-4f4d-b96f-a7d430815603_800x533.webp)

Jakub and Martin sat in the morning at The Italians café. “It’s a paradox,” Jakub says, “instead of opening Photoshop or Figma to draw buttons for an app like before, it pays off to write a small app in Cursor that generates those buttons from given parameters.”

The paradox of building your own tool begins with an unusual clarity: you know exactly what you want. Once the goal is sharp, the main obstacle is no longer technical difficulty but the friction imposed by existing tools. Specialised software promises efficiency but often requires adaptation — learning its logic, navigating the interface and accepting compromises.

AI‑assisted programming shifts the balance. Creating a small, purpose‑built utility can be faster than configuring, taming and bending a ready‑made tool. Intent need not be contorted to fit the tool’s workflow; it is translated directly into behaviour. The resulting script is often fragile and short‑lived, but precise. It does one thing — and does it without negotiation. In that sense it is not a tool in the traditional meaning, but an executable decision.

The paradox deepens when the traditionally simpler choice — using the right tool — requires more effort than quickly building your own utility. General tools carry the weight of many users and future scenarios. The bespoke utility carries a single opinion.

This lightness has its cost. Such tools age poorly, create maintenance debt and tempt work at the meta level. Yet they return authorship, control and even the joy of making. The real shift is not technical but conceptual: the key skill becomes knowing precisely what is worth automating.

## Linkodrome

**Three pillars of great UX: context, usability, playfulness.** *Fred Beecher* explores how playfulness and usability complement one another when designing delightful user experiences. Comparing two iPhone unit converters — Convert and Convertbot — he tested how different levels of playfulness affect the experience. For simple, infrequent tasks, intuitive usability wins; for frequent, looser tasks, playfulness can create a richer experience (see: https://www.fredbeecher.com/writing/2009/playfulness-usability-context-the-three-pillars-of-a-delightful-user-experience).

**How to improve visual design.** *Filippos Protogeridis* summarises proven ways to meaningfully improve your visual design skills: repeatedly study typography, colour and layout, analyse design languages, copy inspiring products in Figma, and regularly revisit older work. Diversity helps — try different styles and product types.

**Dithering: when noise gives an image meaning.** Dithering was originally a practical solution to technical limits — with limited memory and low colour depth developers tricked the eye into seeing more shades. The principle is adding noise between two colours to create the illusion of smooth gradients. Today we no longer need dithering, but it survives as a nostalgic visual style.

**Designers are natural AI orchestrators.** *Benhur Senabathi* argues that designers need not know how to code but must be able to precisely define what they want and why. He outlines four phases: blind reliance on AI, conversational tuning, systems thinking and recognising when to stop tweaking and start testing.

> “The hardest UX problems don’t have interfaces.”
> **— Aneesh Viswanath**

**Experience strategy is a growing discipline in UX.** It aims to align company priorities with what truly improves customers’, users’ and employees’ lives. Jared Spool notes that experience strategists connect user value to business goals, engage leadership and drive innovation.

**Why design leaders feel powerless?** *Peter Merholz* argues that many UX leaders have failed to create the conditions where design can thrive. Instead of leading, they became victims. He calls for taking responsibility, focusing on business outcomes and building credibility.

> “You have to fix the process before you can automate the process. Or else you’re just going to compound the problems.”
> **— Erika Hall**

**Is design review a waste of time?** *Balint Bogdan* criticises vague, aesthetic feedback that doesn’t address whether a design meets user and business goals. He suggests three techniques: clearly define the question, collect feedback individually and separate reactions from proposed solutions. Constructive critique focuses on function, not appearance.

**The CSS property corner‑shape lets you shape element corners more precisely.** Besides classic rounding it supports shapes like scoop, bevel, notch, square, squircle and mathematical `superellipse()`. The property is animatable and lets you set different shapes per corner. Effects such as shadow, background and border adapt to the shape.

**Book** ***Accessibility For Everyone*** **— free online.** Although published in 2017, its core principles — understanding needs of people with disabilities, correct HTML authoring and accessible copy — remain valid. Laura Kalbag has no immediate plans for a new edition.

**Index CSGOV** is a Czech tool that evaluates public websites for accessibility and speed, combining both metrics into a single score. The measurement is regular, automated and transparent, encouraging improvement. Public results show who performs best and where weaknesses lie. The same methodology can be used for private sites.
