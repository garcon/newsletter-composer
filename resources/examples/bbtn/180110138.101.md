## Linkodrome

**Design without process is tempting** because of its speed, but unsustainable in the long term. *Pavel Samsonov* defends friction as a constructive force that gives design meaning, depth and influence. He criticises optimisation at all costs, including blind faith in AI tools that favour superficial solutions. Designers have lost agency by abandoning process in the name of efficiency. Returning to thoughtful process is a way to regain trust and influence in product development – and to turn friction into an advantage.

**UX fails because it still behaves like an agency.** UX doesn’t fail because of Agile; it fails because it still operates like a traditional agency – with preallocated hours, locked decisions and fixed deliverables. *Robert Duebelbeis* argues that UX operates in an old advertising paradigm of “surprise and delight” instead of focusing on learning and value.

![A 1999 print ad for Google promises clean search without weather, news, ads and other portal clutter.](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/ce084942-afc9-4e28-8d32-2e674c765a0b_718x718.jpeg)

**AI killed the homepage.** *Michal Půr* looks back at the end of classic media’s primary traffic sources in 2025 – search engines and social networks. Broad‑interest media are fading; opportunities belong to strong brands, niche specialists or authors with their own audience. Survival depends on trust, context and direct monetisation – subscriptions, sponsorship or video content on global platforms.

**Marketplaces are displacing traditional e‑shops.** Traditional multibrand e‑shops without their own brand or specialisation are losing ground – platforms like Temu, Amazon or Allegro have advantages in price, logistics and marketing. Small Czech shops become unsustainable unless they find a narrow market niche or build a distinctive brand. Regulation in China doesn’t resolve the issue – it merely evens the playing field. Only those with a clear customer proposition will survive.

> “Low‑maturity product orgs fetishise ideas.”
> **— [Pavel Samsonov](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7405365843045552128/)**

**UX research has greater impact when you experience it.** *Kate Kaplan* outlines three types of workshops: alignment with research, building empathy and applying outcomes. She suggests activities such as gallery walks, empathy mapping and “How might we” ideation. The emphasis is on linking insights, emotion and action – and on mixing approaches according to team context.

**Dashboards fail because they’re not actionable.** They overwhelm users with data without clearly indicating what needs attention. *Vitaly Friedman* presents the concept of action dots from *Nick Desbarats* to address this problem. Instead of traditional indicators, use action dots to distinguish crisis, neutral and outstanding metric states. These visual cues are intuitive, easy to read and accessible to colour‑blind users. The result: clearer dashboards that highlight where intervention is required and make everyday decisions easier.

> “The best tech innovation in the last couple of years is not AI, it’s the ‘undo’ button when you just sent an email and noticed you forgot the link/attachment.”
> **— Stéphanie Walter**

**100% accessibility does not exist.** *Adrian Roselli* warns that meeting WCAG 2.2 alone won’t make a product accessible to everyone because accessibility varies across human needs, technologies and contexts. Adrian stresses that accessibility isn’t purely technical and recommends specifying which features make a product more accessible.

**More legible fonts.** Some typefaces include character variants that improve distinction between `Il1` or `0O`. You can prefer more legible variants using low‑level CSS such as:

```css
font-feature-settings: "cv08", "cv05", "zero";
```

Or use `font-variant-alternates` and `font-variant-numeric`.

**How to optimise slow Figma files.** If your Figma files are sluggish, split and slim down libraries. Break central libraries into smaller files, remove hidden layers and unused components, split large components and prefer overrides to heavy variants. Expose shared properties via component properties and simplify SVGs and large assets.

**Add a cat or a dog.** It’s a bit anachronistic in an age of generated images, but if you need placeholders choose between [cats] or [dogs].

## Elizabeth Feinler – the domain pioneer

![Elizabeth Feinler – an illustration generated from a photograph](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/84ae2e3f-e9b2-4cf4-9f2b-b28f7d3fee6d_1536x1024.webp)

*[Elizabeth Feinler](https://www.reknisioweb.cz/p/elizabeth-feinler)* as head of the Network Information Center for ARPANET introduced the WHOIS directory, clarifying who runs internet services. Her team proposed early top‑level domains. The .com domain was originally to be .bus but was changed during DNS implementation.

> “It will be interesting to see whether the internet and computers augment our intelligence and lives, or whether they replace them.”
> **— [Elizabeth Feinler]**

In the early internet Elizabeth maintained a hosts file – yes, the same hosts file you still have on your computer. Others downloaded it from FTP to use the naming system until DNS replaced it in the early 1980s.

She viewed the internet as a social space, not just a technical network. After her career she worked to preserve its history. Her legacy: the internet needs not only technology but also order, responsibility and memory.

## To be clear

Backlinks once decided search rankings, but since 2012 their influence has declined. Search engines now weigh relevance, quality and trust. Backlinks are still useful for discovery and as a secondary trust signal.

The availability heuristic is a cognitive bias where easily recalled experiences are judged as common or important, even when they are not. In UX and team decisions a single strong incident can outweigh stable data. The remedy: record decisions, show trends and balance strong anecdotes with data.

A design challenge is a simple strategic question that turns a vague brief into a concrete creative direction. It helps teams focus on why, not how, and links business goals with user needs.

Edge cases are rare but valid user situations that test resilience. They often reveal failures in extreme conditions – long names, poor connections, stress. Test beyond ideal conditions; edge cases matter.
