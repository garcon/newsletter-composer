## What time took away

One defence against bloat and feature creep is shutting down services that no longer serve a purpose. This year several useful services ended that we will miss.

- [Rise Calendar] (31 March 2025) – a calendar that could block time across all your calendars when an event appeared. Very handy for separating work and personal calendars.
- [Skype] (5 May 2025) – replaced definitively by Teams. We shed a nostalgic tear for the Skype days.
- [Pocket] (8 July 2025) – many services save articles for later now, but Pocket looked good and had useful connectors.
- [Glitch] (8 July 2025) – helped many people put things on the web when complex dev stacks were a barrier.
- [Google URL Shortener] (25 August 2025) – a reminder that trusting big corporations with essential tools can end badly.
- [Meta Workplace Platform] (31 August 2025) – maybe few of us used it, but it was an enterprise groupware alternative.
- [Apple Clips] (10 October 2025) – nice to have a native app in a suite, though we often chose CutCap instead.
- [Windows 10] (14 October 2025) – listed mostly for completeness; the end wasn’t as dramatic as expected.
- [DNS0.eu] (20 October 2025) – a European alternative DNS that closed due to lack of funding.
- [Dropbox Passwords] (28 October 2025) – we gradually migrated away from Dropbox for password management.
- [Google Play Instant] (December 2025) – a handy service that let you run lightweight apps without installation; we’ll miss quick demos.
- [Microsoft Lens] (15 December 2025) – its features are now handled by camera apps and desktop tools.

We’d love to read in the comments which discontinued service of 2025 you’ll miss. You can also mention non‑internet services, like the end of MTV music television.

## Linkodrome

**What did Jakob Nielsen have to (un)learn in 2025?** People are becoming less useful – interventions often worsen AI outputs. Interfaces disappear because AI responds directly to intent. Visualisation is revolutionised by tools like Nano Banana Pro. Pace slows – quality AI conclusions take time. Human value lies more in decision‑making than in skills. Instead of “prompting” we’re navigating latent spaces like exploring an unfamiliar city.

**What makes a design good?** *Sarah Gibbons* and *Kelley Gordon* began a series explaining good practice. Their first piece asks why some designs look good; subsequent articles examine the anatomy of good design and principles of visual design and their influence on perception.

![Meme image]

**What happens if you sketch every day for a month?** *Sarani Mendis* sketched daily and it changed how she thinks about UX: trusting intuition, valuing structure over perfection and accepting errors as part of the process.

**Why do experienced designers move into business roles?** As designers take on greater responsibility, they shift attention from pixels to how a product functions within corporate strategy. Design becomes a decision‑making and optimisation tool rather than mere aesthetics. The shift requires knowledge of business metrics, empathy and systems thinking. Senior designers become strategic partners rather than just interface creators.

**Google Sans, Google’s new corporate typeface,** is now available on [Google Fonts]. After years in Pixel phones and Google apps, its variable features make it broadly useful and open‑source – a big step towards wider accessibility beyond Google’s ecosystem.

**Container queries have advanced since 2022.** They now include scroll‑state queries and anchored queries reacting to element position. They may gain even more capabilities in future.

> Dana Beková reviewed our book and took it to Zákupy castle. We’re grateful for the support and ideas for future volumes.

**Strategy Link Bank** is an extensive open collection of resources for marketing and brand strategists – a goldmine for beginners.

**Is research and testing just cheerleading for design?** Pavel Samsonov criticises practices where research validates preexisting decisions rather than driving meaningful solutions. Research without space to fail or iterate becomes a fig leaf for bad decisions.

> “Coding an app in Shiny, Observable, Streamlit or plain JavaScript is easier and faster for me than clicking in Looker Studio.”
> **— [Marek Prokop]**

**Good URLs don’t change.** Mike Atherton’s case study on domain modelling for the BBC is inspiring. For general guidance, see Queensland’s URL design guidelines.

**What does behavioural design bring to UX practice?** Behavioural design adds a scientific frame to understand why people act as they do and how to nudge behaviour ethically.

## William E. Hick counted why less is more

![William E. Hick image]

William Edmund Hick was a British experimental psychologist who in 1952 showed that decision time grows logarithmically with the number of choices. His work helps quantify human decision‑making and underpins design that accounts for cognitive limits.
